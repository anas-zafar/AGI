Abstract

Can machines truly think, reason and act in domains like humans? This enduring question continues to shape the pursuit of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). Despite the growing capabilities of models such as GPT-4.5, DeepSeek, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Phi-4, and Grok 3, which exhibit multimodal fluency and partial reasoning, these systems remain fundamentally limited by their reliance on token-level prediction and lack grounded agency. This paper offers a cross-disciplinary synthesis of AGI development, spanning artificial intelligence, cognitive neuroscience, psychology, generative models, and agent-based systems. We analyze the architectural and cognitive foundations of general intelligence, highlighting the role of modular reasoning, persistent memory, and multi-agent coordination. In particular, we emphasize the rise of Agentic RAG frameworks that combine retrieval, planning, and dynamic tool use to enable more adaptive behavior. We discuss generalization strategies, including information compression, test-time adaptation, and training-free methods, as critical pathways toward flexible, domain-agnostic intelligence. Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are reexamined not just as perception modules but as evolving interfaces for embodied understanding and collaborative task completion. We also argue that true intelligence arises not from scale alone but from the integration of memory and reasoning: an orchestration of modular, interactive, and self-improving components where compression enables adaptive behavior. Drawing on advances in neurosymbolic systems, reinforcement learning, and cognitive scaffolding, we explore how recent architectures begin to bridge the gap between statistical learning and goal-directed cognition. Finally, we identify key scientific, technical, and ethical challenges on the path to AGI, advocating for systems that are not only intelligent but also transparent, value-aligned, and socially grounded. We anticipate that this paper will serve as a foundational reference for researchers building the next generation of general-purpose human-level machine intelligence.

Introduction

Can machines truly think? Over seven decades ago, Alan Turing famously posed this foundational question at the dawn of computing. It remains central to the field of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), which seeks to replicate the full breadth of human cognitive abilities in computational form. Yet, despite decades of progress, the term "thinking" itself is often invoked without sufficient precision. To meaningfully address this question, we must first define what we mean by thinking and related concepts:

  • Thinking: The manipulation of internal representations to solve problems, reason about the world, and generate novel ideas
  • Consciousness: The subjective capacity for awareness and self-reflection
  • Intelligence: The capacity to acquire, apply, and adapt knowledge across tasks and environments
  • AGI: Systems capable of broad, human-level reasoning and learning across domains, without the need for task-specific retraining

Why Token-level Next-word Prediction Alone is Insufficient for AGI?

Next-token prediction models capture surface linguistic patterns but fail to support complex mental representations grounded in the physical world. Lacking embodiment, causality, and self-reflection, they struggle with abstraction and goal-directed behavior—core requirements for AGI.

While leading-edge AI models such as GPT-4, DeepSeek, and Grok have demonstrated impressive performance across diverse specialized tasks, their underlying architecture remains fundamentally limited by token-level prediction. Although this paradigm excels at surface-level pattern recognition, it lacks grounding in physical embodiment, higher-order reasoning, and reflective self-awareness, which are core attributes of general intelligence.

Research Approach

This survey employs a cross-disciplinary synthesis methodology, examining AGI development through multiple theoretical and practical lenses. Our approach systematically analyzes the convergence of insights from:

  • Artificial Intelligence: Current architectures, limitations, and emerging paradigms in foundation models
  • Cognitive Neuroscience: Brain-inspired models, neural computation principles, and memory systems
  • Psychology: Cognitive mechanisms, learning processes, and human-AI interaction paradigms
  • Generative Models: Large language models, vision-language models, and their architectural constraints
  • Agent-Based Systems: Multi-agent coordination, agentic AI, and emergent intelligence
  • Neurosymbolic Systems: Integration of symbolic reasoning and connectionist approaches

Key Methodological Frameworks

We organize our analysis around three core dimensions:

Architectural Analysis

Systematic comparison of current AI architectures with cognitive models from neuroscience and psychology

Capability Assessment

Evaluation of current systems against requirements for general intelligence and human-level reasoning

Future Pathways

Identification of promising research directions, integration strategies, and societal implications

Key Findings

Our cross-disciplinary analysis reveals several critical insights about the current state and future directions of AGI research:

1. Fundamental Limitations of Current Token-Based Models

  • Token-level prediction constraints: Limited to surface linguistic patterns without grounded understanding
  • Lack of embodied cognition: No physical grounding or causal reasoning capabilities
  • Memory limitations: Insufficient persistent memory and cross-domain generalization
  • Absent higher-order reasoning: No self-reflection, meta-cognition, or abstract thinking

2. Promising Pathways Toward AGI

🤖 Agentic RAG Frameworks

Integration of retrieval, planning, and dynamic tool use to enable more adaptive behavior beyond static prediction

🧠 Modular Reasoning Architectures

Brain-inspired modular cognitive systems that mirror the specialized yet integrated nature of human intelligence

💾 Persistent Memory Systems

Long-term knowledge retention and adaptation mechanisms enabling continuous learning and context preservation

🤝 Multi-Agent Coordination

Collaborative intelligence through agent interaction, enabling distributed problem-solving and collective intelligence

3. Critical Generalization Strategies

  • Information Compression: Efficient representation learning for adaptive behavior and knowledge transfer
  • Test-Time Adaptation: Dynamic adjustment to novel scenarios without extensive retraining
  • Training-Free Methods: Zero-shot generalization across domains and tasks
  • Neurosymbolic Integration: Bridging statistical learning and symbolic reasoning for robust cognition

4. Vision-Language Models as Cognitive Interfaces

VLMs represent a critical evolution beyond simple perception modules, serving as interfaces for embodied understanding and collaborative task completion. These systems demonstrate the potential for multimodal integration that approximates human-like cognitive processing and real-world interaction.

Conclusion

AGI remains one of the most profound scientific challenges of our time, demanding not only greater scale, but also deeper alignment with the cognitive, ethical, and societal foundations of human intelligence. This paper has examined AGI from a multidisciplinary lens, synthesizing insights from neuroscience, symbolic reasoning, learning theory, and social systems design.

Key Insights and Implications

  • Beyond Scale: True intelligence emerges from integration of modular, interactive, and self-improving components rather than parameter scaling alone
  • Grounded Intelligence: AGI requires grounded world models, dynamic memory, and causal reasoning capabilities
  • Agentic Evolution: The transition from static models to agentic systems represents a paradigm shift toward human-like cognition
  • Ethical Integration: Safe AGI deployment requires proactive ethical frameworks and value alignment from the outset

Future Research Directions

Several critical challenges remain on the path to AGI:

  • Developing robust uncertainty handling for both epistemic and aleatory uncertainty
  • Creating emotional and social understanding capabilities for human-AI interaction
  • Establishing comprehensive ethical and moral judgment frameworks
  • Addressing cognitive and technical debt in AI system development
  • Minimizing environmental impact and power consumption

Looking forward, we believe that true progress toward AGI will require a fundamental shift from monolithic models to modular, self-adaptive, and value-aligned systems. This transition must be accompanied by social foresight, involving the proactive redesign of education, labor, and policy frameworks to accommodate and co-evolve with intelligent machines.

Final Reflection: AGI cannot be purely a technical pursuit. It must be a human project with development progressing alongside humans actively involved in the process. We anticipate that this paper will serve as a foundational reference for researchers building the next generation of general-purpose human-level machine intelligence.
